Common continuous distributions¶

A continuous probability distribution is specified by a probability density function $f$ which can be integrated to find probabilities of events. $$ P(A) = \int_A f(x)\, dx $$ The density $f(x)$ at a point $x$ is not a probability. A continuous distribution assigns a probability of zero to all values, but $$ P([x - h/2, x + h/2]) \approx f(x)h $$

Uniform distributions are used to model situations is which every value in an interval is "equally likely". The density of a Uniform distribution with parameters $a$ and $b$ is constant on the interval $(a, b)$ and is specified by the probability density function $$ f(x) = \frac{1}{b-a}, \quad a < x < b $$ If $X$ is a random variable with a Uniform($a, b$) distribution then $$ \begin{align*} E(X) & = \frac{a + b}{2} \\ Var(X) & = \frac{(b - a)^2}{12} \end{align*} $$

A Normal (a.k.a. Gaussian) distribution with mean $\mu$ and standard deviation $\sigma>0$ (and variance $\sigma^2$) has the probability density function $$ f(x) = \frac{1}{\sigma \sqrt{2\pi}} \exp\left({-\frac{1}{2}\left(\frac{x-\mu}{\sigma}\right)^2}\right), \quad -\infty < x< \infty $$ If $X$ is a random variable with a Normal($\mu$ , $\sigma$ ) distribution then $$ \begin{align*} E(X) & = \mu \\ Var(X) & = \sigma^2 \end{align*} $$

A LogNormal distribution with parameters $\mu \in \mathbb{R}$ and $\sigma \in \mathbb{R}$ is specified by the probability density function

$$ f(x) = \frac{1}{\left(\sigma x \sqrt{2\pi}\right)} e^{\left(-1/2\right)\left(\frac{log(x)}{\sigma}\right)^2} $$

If $Y$ has a LogNormal($\mu,\,\sigma$), then log($Y$) has a normal distribution with mean $\mu$ and sd $\sigma$.

An Exponential distribution with parameter $\lambda>0$ is specified by the probability density function $$ f(x) = \lambda e^{-\lambda x}, \quad x>0 $$ Exponential distributions can be parametrized in terms of the rate parameter $\lambda$ (using rate=) or the scale parameter $1/\lambda$ (using scale=).
If $X$ is a random variable with a Exponential($\lambda$) distribution then $$ \begin{align*} E(X) & = \frac{1}{\lambda} \\ Var(X) & = \frac{1}{\lambda^2} \end{align*} $$

A Gamma distribution with shape parameter $\alpha>0$ and rate parameter $\lambda >0$ has density $$ f(x) = \frac{\lambda^\alpha}{\Gamma(\alpha)} e^{-\lambda x} x^{\alpha -1}, \quad x>0 $$ where $$ \Gamma(\alpha) = \int_0^\infty e^{-u}u^{\alpha - 1}\, du, \quad \text{with } \Gamma(k) = (k-1)! \text{ if $k$ is a positive integer} $$ Gamma distributions can be parametrized in terms of the rate parameter $\lambda$ (using rate=) or the scale parameter $1/\lambda$ (using scale=). If $X$ is a random variable with a Gamma($\alpha, \lambda$) distribution then $$ \begin{align*} E(X) & = \frac{\alpha}{\lambda} \\ Var(X) & = \frac{\alpha}{\lambda^2} \end{align*} $$

A Gamma distribution with shape parameter $\frac{m}{2}$ and rate parameter $\frac{1}{2}$ is also known as a Chi-square distribution with m degrees of freedom and has density

$$ f(x) = \frac{\frac{1}{2}^{m/2}}{\Gamma\left(\frac{m}{2}\right)} e^{-\left(1/2\right)x} \, x^{ m/2 - 1}, \quad x>0 $$

If $X$ is a random variable with a ChiSquare($m$) distribution then $$ \begin{align*} E(X) & = m \\ Var(X) & = 2m \end{align*} $$

A StudentT distribution with m degrees of freedom has the probability density function

$$ f(x) = \frac{\Gamma \left(\frac{m+1}{2}\right)}{\sqrt{m\pi}\,\Gamma\left(\frac{m}{2}\right)} \left(1+x^{2}/m\right)^{-\left(m+1\right)/2} $$

If $X$ is a random variable with a StudentT($m$) distribution then $$ \begin{align*} E(X) & = 0 \\ Var(X) & = \frac{m}{m - 2} \end{align*} $$

A StudentT distribution is more conveniently defined via the following:

If Z has a Normal(0,1) distribution, Y has a chi-square distribution with m degrees of freedom, and are Y and Z independent, then the random variable

$$\frac{Z}{ \sqrt{Y/m} }$$

has a StudentT distribution with m degrees of freedom.

The pdf of the StudentT distribution with 1 degree of freedom is also known as the Cauchy distribution with the probability density function

$$ f(x) = \frac{1}{\pi} \frac{1}{1 + x^2} $$

The probability density above is defined in the “standardized” form. To shift and/or scale the distribution use the location parameter $x_0 \in \mathbb{R}$ (loc =) and scale parameter $\gamma \in \mathbb{R}$ (scale =)

If $X$ is a random variable with a Cauchy($x_0,\,\gamma$) distribution then $$ \begin{align*} E(X) & \text{ is undefined} \\ Var(X) & \text{ is undefined} \end{align*} $$

An F distribution with m degrees of freedom in the numerator and n degrees of freedom in the denominator has the probability density function

$$ f(x) = \frac{\Gamma\left(\left(m + n\right)/2\right)m^{m/2}n^{n/2}}{\Gamma\left(m/2\right)\Gamma\left(n/2\right)} \frac{x^{m/2 - 1}}{\left(mx + n\right)^{\left(m + n\right)/2}}, \quad x > 0 $$

If $X$ is a random variable with a F($m, n$) distribution then $$ \begin{align*} E(X) & = \frac{n}{n - 2} \qquad \text{for} \, n > 2 \\ Var(X) & = \frac{2m^2(m + n - 2)}{m(n-2)^2(n-4)} \qquad \text{for} \, n > 4 \end{align*} $$

An F distribution is more conviently defined via the following:

If $Y_1$ has a chi-square distribution with m degrees of freedom, $Y_2$ has a chi-square distribution with n degress of freedom, and $Y_1$ and $Y_2$ are independent, then the random variable

$$\frac{Y_1/m}{Y_2/n}$$

has an F distribution with m degrees of freedom in the numerator and n degrees of freedom in the denominator.

Beta distributions are probability distributions on bounded intervals. For a Uniform(0, 1) distribution, the probability density is constant on the interval (0, 1). Beta distributions are probability distributions on the interval (0, 1) where the density is not necessarily constant. A Beta distribution with parameters $\alpha>0$ and $\beta>0$ has the probability density function $$ f(x) = \frac{\Gamma(\alpha + \beta)}{\Gamma(\alpha)\Gamma(\beta)} x^{\alpha-1} (1-x)^{\beta-1}, \qquad 0 < x < 1, $$ where $$ \Gamma(\alpha) = \int_0^\infty e^{-u}u^{\alpha - 1}\, du, \quad \text{with } \Gamma(k) = (k-1)! \text{ if $k$ is a positive integer} $$ Note that the Beta distribution with $\alpha = \beta=1$ is the Uniform(0, 1) distribution.

If $X$ is a random variable with a Beta($\alpha, \beta$) distribution then $$ \begin{align*} E(X) & = \frac{\alpha}{\alpha + \beta} \\ Var(X) & = \frac{\alpha\beta}{(\alpha + \beta)^2(\alpha + \beta + 1)} \end{align*} $$

A Rayleigh distribution is a special case of ChiSquare with df = 2 with probability density function

$$ f(x) = x e^{-x^2/2}, \qquad x \geq 0 $$

The Rayleigh distribution has no parameters.

If $X$ is a random variable with a Rayleigh() distribution then $$ \begin{align*} E(X) & = \sqrt{\frac{\pi}{2}}\\ Var(X) & = \frac{4 - \pi}{2} \end{align*} $$

A Pareto distribution takes b as a shape paremater with the probability density function

$$ f(x) = \frac{b}{x^{(b + 1)}}, \qquad x \geq 1, \, b > 0 $$

If $X$ is a random variable with a Pareto(b) distribution then $$ \begin{align*} E(X) & = \frac{b}{b - 1} \text{ if } b > 1 \\ Var(X) & = \frac{b}{(b - 1)^2(b - 2)} \text{ if } b > 2 \end{align*} $$
